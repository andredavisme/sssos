Subject Glossary

System
The organized set of components within any known entity that operates to produce results.
Entity
Any physical or metaphysical subject that contains or constitutes a system.
Observer
The individual who defines necessity, establishes standards, and evaluates a system's outputs.
Standard
The mathematical average of granular components across all subjects known to the observer.
Necessity
The requirement for a system to exist or operate, defined entirely by the observer.
Complex
A system whose granular component count exceeds the observer's standard.
Simple
A system whose granular component count falls below the observer's standard.
Granular Component
An individual sub-unit within a system; the discrete parts that together form the whole.
Priority Entity
The intended result or output a system is expected to consistently produce.
Physical System
A system composed of material objects of any size that are comprised of atoms.
Metaphysical System
A system composed of concepts and representations of those concepts expressed as communicated ideas.
Undesired Bias
Any element in a system's construction that skews output away from the observer's defined necessity.
Optimization
The process of refining a system to consistently produce its priority entities and resist undesired bias.
Personal Experience Reflection
A recorded, written observation of one's history with a system, used to refine perspective and identify bias over time.

Section 5 · Chapter 1

What Is a System?

Before we can observe, measure, or optimize a system, we must be able to recognize one. This chapter establishes the four foundational definitions that underpin every concept in this subject.

5a · Granular Subject Matter

A system is the organized set of components within any known entity that operates to produce results. This definition applies universally — to objects, organisms, documents, relationships, and institutions alike.

An entity is any subject — physical or metaphysical — that contains or constitutes a system. The entity is the whole; the system is the operational structure inside it.

Physical Entity

A physical entity is a material object of any size comprised of atoms. Physical entities occupy space and have observable, measurable components.

  • Human Body
  • Dog Body
  • Gorilla Body
  • Dolphin Body
  • Toaster Oven

Metaphysical Entity

A metaphysical entity is a concept or the representation of a concept, expressed as a communicated idea. Metaphysical entities do not occupy physical space — they exist as structure, meaning, and agreement.

  • Family Tree
  • Course Syllabus
  • Business Procedure
  • Emergency Escape Plan
  • Government Infrastructure

Key Principle

Every known entity — whether physical or metaphysical — contains a system. This is not a claim that all systems are visible or easy to map. It is a claim that the structure exists. The observer's task is to find it.

5b · Practical Application

Recognizing systems in practice means learning to ask two questions of any entity you encounter: What are its components? and What is it supposed to produce?

Physical: The Human Body

The human body is a physical entity. Its system includes organs, tissues, skeletal structures, and biochemical processes. Each component contributes to the body's priority entities — movement, cognition, respiration, reproduction. When a component fails, the system produces inconsistent or absent results.

Metaphysical: A Course Syllabus

A course syllabus is a metaphysical entity. Its system includes learning objectives, assigned readings, assessment schedules, and participation expectations. Its priority entity is a prepared, informed student. When a component is vague or absent, the system underperforms.

The Pattern Across Both Types

Whether the entity is physical or metaphysical, the same logic applies: components are organized to produce results. The observer who can see this pattern in a toaster oven can also see it in a government department — because the structure of observation is the same.

5c · Analogous Application

An analogy is a side-by-side comparison of two systems that share structural similarities despite belonging to different categories. Analogies are one of the most powerful tools an observer has — they reveal which design choices are universal and which are specific to a single entity's necessity.

Analogy: Human Body ↔ Business Procedure

Both are systems. Both have components with defined roles. Both produce outputs — health and function vs. completed tasks and decisions. Both can develop "disease" (undesired bias): a body can develop a condition that disrupts normal output; a procedure can develop redundant approval steps that stall its own results.

The analogy does not collapse the difference between physical and metaphysical. It reveals that the logic of systems operates the same way across both categories.

Analogy: Toaster Oven ↔ Family Tree

A toaster oven is a physical system: components (heating element, timer, thermostat) produce a result (evenly toasted food). A family tree is a metaphysical system: components (names, relationships, generational lines) produce a result (a navigable record of lineage). Both can be simple or complex depending on the observer's standard — and both can have missing components that impair output.

5d · Personal Application

Now apply the definitions to your own experience. Choose one entity you interact with regularly — physical or metaphysical. Identify it as one or the other, name its system, and describe what it is supposed to produce.

5e · Personal Experience Reflection

Think of a time you encountered a system — physical or metaphysical — and did not recognize it as a system at the time. You may have experienced it as confusion, frustration, or simply as "the way things are." Looking back, can you now name its components and what it was trying to produce?

5f · Current Events Relationship

Systems are not historical artifacts — they are operating right now, in the world around you. Identify a current event or ongoing situation in the news, your community, or your workplace. Name the entity at the center of it. Is it physical or metaphysical? What system does it contain, and what was that system supposed to produce?

Section 12

Subject Conclusion

Every known entity contains a system. Systems are as complex or simple as is necessary — and necessity is defined by you, the observer.

Through this subject you have encountered the definitions, examples, and reflective tools to begin observing the systems you inhabit and the systems you wish to optimize. The standard by which you measure complexity is your own — and it will grow as your knowledge grows.

What you protect against is undesired bias: the subtle distortions in a system's construction that prevent it from consistently producing its priority entities. The antidote is perspective — developed through discussion, reflection, and honest observation.

You are the observer. You define the necessity. You set the standard. You determine what the system is for.
