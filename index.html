Introduction

Section 4

Subject Glossary

System
The organized set of components within any known entity that operates to produce results.
Entity
Any physical or metaphysical subject that contains or constitutes a system.
Observer
The individual who defines necessity, establishes standards, and evaluates a system's outputs.
Standard
The mathematical average of granular components across all subjects known to the observer.
Necessity
The requirement for a system to exist or operate, defined entirely by the observer.
Complex
A system whose granular component count exceeds the observer's standard.
Simple
A system whose granular component count falls below the observer's standard.
Granular Component
An individual sub-unit within a system; the discrete parts that together form the whole.
Priority Entity
The intended result or output a system is expected to consistently produce.
Physical System
A system composed of material objects of any size that are comprised of atoms.
Metaphysical System
A system composed of concepts and representations of those concepts expressed as communicated ideas.
Undesired Bias
Any element in a system's construction that skews output away from the observer's defined necessity.
Optimization
The process of refining a system to consistently produce its priority entities and resist undesired bias.
Personal Experience Reflection
A recorded, written observation of one's history with a system, used to refine perspective and identify bias over time.

Section 5

Subject Content

5a · Granular Subject Matter

Every system can be decomposed into its granular components — the discrete sub-units that, together, form the whole. The count and arrangement of these components determine whether a system is observed as simple or complex relative to the observer's personal standard.

The observer's standard is not fixed. It is the mathematical average of all subjects the observer currently knows. As knowledge grows, the standard shifts — and so does the threshold between simple and complex.

Example Calculation

If an observer knows five systems with the following estimated component counts:

  • Human Body: 1,000
  • Toaster Oven: 50
  • Family Tree: 20
  • Course Syllabus: 15
  • Business Procedure: 30

Standard = (1000 + 50 + 20 + 15 + 30) ÷ 5 = 223

Any new system with more than 223 components is complex; fewer than 223 is simple.

5b · Practical Application

Practical application means identifying how a system's granular components function in a real-world setting. The observer looks at the system as it actually operates — not as it is theoretically designed — and evaluates whether each component contributes to the production of priority entities.

Physical: Toaster Oven

Components include the heating element, timer, thermostat, and exterior casing. In practice, the priority entity is evenly toasted food. If heat distribution is uneven, the system has a bias against its own necessity.

Metaphysical: Business Procedure

Components include approval steps, signatories, and timelines. In practice, the priority entity is a consistently completed task. If one department requires five approvals and another one, the procedure has an undesired bias.

5c · Analogous Application

Analogous application compares a system's components to those of similar entities. This process expands the observer's standard — each new analogy adds to the mathematical average — and reveals whether the system under observation deviates meaningfully from peers in its category.

Analogy: Emergency Escape Plan ↔ Course Syllabus

Both are metaphysical systems. Both have sequential steps, defined roles, and an intended outcome (safety vs. learning). Comparing their granular components — routes and muster points vs. modules and assessments — clarifies which design choices are category-standard and which are unique to each system's necessity.

5d · Personal Application

Personal application asks the observer to identify how they personally interface with a system's components. This is the bridge between abstract observation and lived experience. The observer does not simply describe the system — they locate themselves within it.

5e · Personal Experience Reflection

This stage asks the observer to look backward — to draw on their unique history with a specific system and record what the intended output was in that context. By writing these reflections down, observers create a permanent reference that evolves alongside their complexity standard.

5f · Current Events Relationship

No system exists in isolation. Every system is influenced by and interacts with the broader world. This stage asks the observer to identify a current event — something happening now — and map it to a system from this subject. How does that event affect the system's granular components, its standard, or its priority entities?

Section 6

Subject Conclusion

Every known entity contains a system. Systems are as complex or simple as is necessary — and necessity is defined by you, the observer.

Through this subject you have encountered the definitions, examples, and reflective tools to begin observing the systems you inhabit and the systems you wish to optimize. The standard by which you measure complexity is your own — and it will grow as your knowledge grows.

What you protect against is undesired bias: the subtle distortions in a system's construction that prevent it from consistently producing its priority entities. The antidote is perspective — developed through discussion, reflection, and honest observation.

You are the observer. You define the necessity. You set the standard. You determine what the system is for.
